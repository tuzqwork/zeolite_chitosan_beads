Results

1. Morphology, Microstructure, and Porosity of the Aerogel Beads

The preparation protocol resulted in pretty spherical beads with increasing size distributions respective of ZX content. The average diameter of beads ranges from ∼1.41 mm (CS-ZX sample at φZX = 1) to ∼ 2.14 mm (CS-ZX sample at φZX = 9). This is likely due to the amount of zeolite connected to the matrix of chitosan – acting as a binding agent. .

2. CO2 adsorption ability


The concentration fraction of CO2 (C/Co) measured at sensor is plotted as a function of time (sec). Since the CO2 adsorption capacity of the pure CS aerogel is below instrument resolution, the remarkable CO2 uptake of the CS-ZX samples can be primarily ascribed to zeolite, and in fact increases with φZX. However, when the zeolite concentration increases, the beads become less porous due to lack of pores and CO2 cannot be held. This leads to a sharp decrease considering C1Z2 versus C1Z4.

Our figure presents the adsorption lines for 4 samples as stated. The CO2 capacity of a sample is evaluated by the area restricted by the curve line, Oy axis and y = 1.
Among the samples, C1Z2 is probably the best one although the differences are not considerable. The CO2 capacity increases with zeolite concentration when observing C1Z1, C1Z4, C1Z9. .

Conclusion


Zeolite X powder was successfully embedded in a chitosan framework to obtain composite aerogel beads by a phase inversion method followed by freezing plus vaporizing solvent.
The beads exhibited excellent CO2 uptake capacity owing to an optimal dispersion of the zeolite powder.
Besides providing mechanical coherence, chitosan likely played an active role in CO2 capture when present at low concentration.
This can be due to an enhanced exposure of the amino groups that occurs when the polymer is highly confined among zeolite microparticles.
